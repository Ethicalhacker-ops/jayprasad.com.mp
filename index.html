About the Nepali Calendar

The Nepali calendar (Bikram Sambat) is the official calendar of Nepal. It is a solar calendar that is approximately 56.7 years ahead of the Gregorian calendar.

Key features:

  • New Year begins in mid-April (usually April 13-15)
  • Months have 28-32 days (unlike Gregorian months)
  • Based on ancient Hindu timekeeping systems
  • Used for all official purposes in Nepal

Country Overview

Nepal, officially the Federal Democratic Republic of Nepal, is a landlocked country in South Asia. It is located mainly in the Himalayas but also includes parts of the Indo-Gangetic Plain. With an estimated population of 30 million, Nepal is the 49th largest country by population and 93rd largest country by area.

Key Facts

  • Capital: Kathmandu
  • Official Language: Nepali
  • Area: 147,516 km² (56,956 sq mi)
  • Population: ~30 million (2023 est.)
  • Currency: Nepalese rupee (NPR)
  • Time Zone: NPT (UTC+05:45)
  • Calling Code: +977
  • Driving Side: Left

Government

Nepal is a federal democratic republic with a multi-party system. The current constitution was adopted in 2015, establishing Nepal as a secular federal parliamentary republic divided into seven provinces.

Geography of Nepal

Nepal is located between China (Tibet Autonomous Region) to the north and India to the south, east, and west. The country can be divided into three main geographical regions:

Himalayan Region

This region contains some of the world's highest mountains, including Mount Everest (8,848m), the highest point on Earth. Eight of the ten highest mountains in the world are located in Nepal.

Hill Region

The central part of Nepal consists of the Mahabharat Range and the Lower Himalayan Range, with elevations ranging from 1,000 to 4,000 meters. This region includes the Kathmandu Valley, the most developed urban area in Nepal.

Terai Region

The southern lowland plains border India and are part of the northern rim of the Indo-Gangetic Plain. This fertile region is the breadbasket of Nepal and contains significant biodiversity.

Climate

Nepal has five climatic zones corresponding to altitude: tropical, subtropical, temperate, subalpine, and alpine. The country experiences four main seasons: spring, summer, monsoon, and winter.

History of Nepal

Ancient Period

The earliest inhabitants of Nepal were the Kirat people, who ruled the Kathmandu Valley from around 800 BCE. The Licchavi dynasty (400-750 CE) introduced Hinduism and established many cultural traditions.

Medieval Period

The Malla dynasty (1200-1768) divided the Kathmandu Valley into three competing kingdoms (Kathmandu, Patan, and Bhaktapur). This period saw great achievements in art, architecture, and literature.

Unification and Shah Dynasty

Prithvi Narayan Shah unified Nepal in 1768, establishing the Shah dynasty that would rule until 2008. The Anglo-Nepalese War (1814-1816) resulted in the Treaty of Sugauli, establishing Nepal's current boundaries.

Modern Period

Nepal transitioned from an absolute monarchy to a constitutional monarchy in 1990. A decade-long civil war (1996-2006) ended with the abolition of the monarchy in 2008 and establishment of a federal democratic republic.

Culture of Nepal

Religion

Nepal is a secular country with Hinduism (81.3%) and Buddhism (9.0%) as the major religions. The country is birthplace of Buddha (Lumbini) and has many important Hindu and Buddhist pilgrimage sites.

Languages

Nepali is the official language, spoken by 44.6% of the population. There are 123 recognized languages, including Maithili, Bhojpuri, Tharu, Tamang, and Newari.

Cuisine

Nepali cuisine is diverse, with dal bhat (lentil soup with rice) as the staple food. Other popular dishes include momo (dumplings), sel roti (rice doughnut), and gundruk (fermented leafy greens).

Festivals

Major festivals include Dashain (longest Hindu festival), Tihar (festival of lights), Holi (festival of colors), Buddha Jayanti (Buddha's birthday), and Teej (women's festival).

Economy of Nepal

Overview

Nepal is a developing country with an agriculture-based economy (24% of GDP). Remittances account for about 25% of GDP, with many Nepalis working abroad, mainly in India, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia.

Key Sectors

  • Agriculture: Employs about 65% of workforce (rice, maize, wheat, sugarcane)
  • Industry: Mainly small-scale (textiles, carpets, cement, bricks)
  • Services: Tourism, retail, transportation, and financial services

Tourism

Tourism is a major source of foreign exchange, with trekking, mountaineering, and religious pilgrimage as key attractions. Nepal receives about 1 million tourists annually (pre-pandemic).

Challenges

Nepal faces challenges including political instability, inadequate infrastructure, corruption, and vulnerability to natural disasters (earthquakes, floods).
